Track A: Material Analysis

Step 1: Mineral Tests

The first step in identifying ivory involves testing its mineral composition. Genuine ivory contains specific mineral signatures that distinguish it from synthetic materials.

Key Points:
  • Ivory is composed primarily of dentine and calcium phosphate
  • Chemical tests can reveal organic vs. inorganic materials
  • UV light testing shows characteristic fluorescence

Step 2: Microscopy

Under microscopic examination, ivory reveals distinctive patterns called Schreger lines - unique crosshatch patterns visible in cross-sections.

What We Look For:
  • Schreger lines (angles and patterns)
  • Growth rings and layers
  • Cell structure and organization
  • Surface texture and grain

Step 3: Stable Isotopes

Stable isotope analysis provides crucial information about the geographic origin and age of ivory specimens.

Analysis Methods:
  • Carbon-14 dating for age determination
  • Strontium isotopes reveal geographic origin
  • Nitrogen isotopes indicate diet and environment
  • Can distinguish pre-ban vs. post-ban ivory

Track B: Object Typology

Piano Keys

üéπ

Historic pianos often featured ivory keys valued for their smooth texture and appearance. Most piano keys were made from African elephant ivory before synthetic alternatives became available.

Identification Features:
  • Thin veneer typically 2-3mm thick
  • Yellowing with age is natural
  • Grain patterns visible under magnification

Combs

üíà

Decorative and functional combs were popular ivory items, prized for their durability and ability to hold intricate carved designs.

Identification Features:
  • Often feature carved decorative patterns
  • Teeth show wear patterns from use
  • May include inlaid materials

Military Insignia

Ivory was used in military insignia, badges, and ceremonial items, particularly in colonial-era military contexts.

Identification Features:
  • Often combined with metals or other materials
  • May bear engraved symbols or text
  • Typically smaller, precision-carved pieces

Carvings

üóø

Artistic carvings represent some of the most valuable and culturally significant ivory objects, ranging from religious figures to decorative sculptures.

Identification Features:
  • Show natural curvature of tusk
  • May display cultural or religious motifs
  • Patina development indicates age
  • Tool marks reveal carving techniques

Track C: Trade History

Maps & Colonial Networks

The ivory trade followed colonial expansion routes, with major hubs in coastal Africa, Asia, and European ports.

Key Trade Routes:
  • East African coast to Asia via Indian Ocean
  • Central Africa to European colonies
  • Trans-Saharan routes to Mediterranean
  • Colonial administrative centers as collection points
These networks reflect broader patterns of colonial exploitation and resource extraction that had lasting impacts on local communities and ecosystems.

Oral Histories

Community oral histories provide essential context often missing from written records, revealing the human impact of the ivory trade.

What Oral Histories Tell Us:
  • Local knowledge of elephant populations and migration
  • Community relationships with wildlife
  • Impact of trade on social structures
  • Traditional craftsmanship and techniques
  • Stories of resistance and adaptation
These narratives are crucial for understanding the full context of ivory objects and their cultural significance.

Shipping Logs

Historical shipping records document the movement of ivory through official trade channels, providing provenance information.

What Shipping Logs Reveal:
  • Quantities and dates of ivory shipments
  • Origin and destination ports
  • Trading companies and merchants involved
  • Market values and economic data
  • Changes in trade patterns over time
Cross-referencing shipping logs with other historical documents helps establish provenance and legality of ivory objects.

Track D: Ethics & Law

CITES Regulations

üìú

The Convention on International Trade in Endangered Species (CITES) regulates ivory trade to protect elephant populations.

Key CITES Provisions:
  • African elephants listed in Appendix I (1989) - commercial trade banned
  • Pre-convention ivory (before 1976) may be legal with documentation
  • Antique ivory (>100 years old) has different regulations
  • All international movement requires permits
  • Penalties for illegal trade include fines and imprisonment

Local Historical Society Stewardship

Historical societies play a critical role in preserving ivory artifacts while respecting ethical considerations.

Stewardship Responsibilities:
  • Proper documentation and provenance research
  • Secure storage and conservation
  • Educational use vs. public display decisions
  • Collaboration with source communities
  • Transparent policies on acquisition and deaccession
Good stewardship means acknowledging the complex history of ivory objects while using them to educate about conservation and historical injustices.

Repatriation Questions

üîÑ

Repatriation of ivory objects raises complex questions about ownership, cultural heritage, and historical justice.

Key Considerations:
  • Determining rightful ownership and cultural affiliation
  • Balancing conservation with community access
  • Addressing colonial-era acquisitions
  • Sacred or ceremonial objects require special consideration
  • Building collaborative relationships with source communities
Reflection Questions:
  • Who has the right to possess culturally significant objects?
  • How do we address historical wrongs while preserving knowledge?
  • What role should descendant communities play in decisions?
  • How can institutions move from ownership to partnership?
